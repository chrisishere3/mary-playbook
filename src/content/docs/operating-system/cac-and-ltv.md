The CAC:LTV ratio is the single most important number for marketing here. We have significant headroom to spend more on acquisition — the question is on which channels.

## CAC calculation

Every quarter:

1. Sum marketing + sales + onboarding spend (media + labor)
2. Divide by homes activated → cost per door

We measure CAC per door, not per partner. A 100-door partner and a 1,000-door partner aren't worth the same.

## Channel grading principle

**Goal: most doors for the cheapest price.**

The trap: channels that produce demo volume but don't convert to activation. Always look at the **activated** stage, not just demos. Some channels look great at the top of the funnel and quietly burn budget downstream.

## LTV assumptions

- **Average partner retention:** 18 months. Conservative — bigger partners do better than this.
- **Lease rate:** not every home that goes on market gets leased. Tear doors down accordingly.
- **Monetization rate:** 25% of moves (purchase rate).

## LTV levers

- Higher gross profit margin over time (operational efficiency)
- Higher revenue per PM (new monetization, better attach rate)

Both compound. Either one moves LTV directly.

- **Current CAC:LTV ratio:** ~7x (minimum required: 3x)

## What this means for marketing

We are not CAC-constrained today. We are under-spending on acquisition by a wide margin. The case for any new channel test isn't "can we afford it" — it's "does it actually produce activated doors at a payback we can defend."

## Where there's more room

- **Cost per activated door, not cost per MQL.** Today we track CPL per channel. Activated doors is the real economic measure. We have the data, just don't use it consistently.
- **Channels at the top of the rank.** If email and direct mail are both producing well below allowable CAC, they're underfunded.
- **Channels nobody owns yet.** SEO, content, and the SFR/PM creator ecosystem (Peter Lohmann is a single example of a category) are all under-tested.
