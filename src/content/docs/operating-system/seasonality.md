Seasonality is the silent biggest lever. In some years it can drive more revenue change than partner growth.

## How seasonality works in our model

- Apply a seasonality curve to expected leases
- Curve < 100% = drag on leases
- Curve > 100% = tailwind

The same partner base produces meaningfully different revenue in Q1 vs Q3 — because tenants don't move uniformly across the year.

## The pattern through the year

- **Jan–May:** below baseline (drag)
- **May–Sep:** above baseline (peak leasing season)
- **Fall:** returns toward baseline
- **December:** lowest point
- **Q1:** often kicks around the same figure

## Why it matters

A flat partner count moving from May to September can look like growth. A growing partner count moving from October to December can look flat. Don't confuse momentum with the curve.

When forecasting:

- Quote partner growth and seasonal lift as separate numbers
- Compare same-month year-over-year, not month-over-month
- A bad December isn't a bad business

## Where there's more room

- **Live seasonality chart.** Today the curve is in someone's head and a spreadsheet. A live Metabase chart that overlays partner growth and seasonal lift would make this conversation easier in every monthly review.
- **Pre-season demand pull.** If May–Sep is peak leasing, the right marketing moment is March/April — partners who activate then capture a full peak season. Outbound and PR cadences could be tuned to that window.
