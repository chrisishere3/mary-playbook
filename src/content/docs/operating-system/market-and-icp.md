The market is third-party property managers. The ideal partner is a mom-and-pop SFR PM in the Sunbelt managing somewhere between 100 and 850 doors. Below 100 doors is currently outside the active motion. **Whether that's strategically correct is one of the open questions** — see [Strategic Disagreements](/mary-playbook/operating-system/strategic-disagreements/).

## Market size

### SFR (1–4 units)

- **22 million total homes** (SFR detached + duplexes + 4-plexes)
- **15 million** if you strip out duplexes and 4-plexes

### Why SFR first

A property manager is a single decision point influencing hundreds of homes. One sale = leverage across all their doors. That's why the SFR PM motion has been the spine of the business.

### Segmentation

- **Third-party PMs:** 36% of the SFR space
- **Self-managed / DIY landlords:** 64%

### Current go-to-market target within SFR

UP actively pursues PMs that manage **100 or more doors**, or that UP believes manage 100+ doors. The 50–100 door band is outside active pursuit but less clearly defined. **Sub-50 doors (the long tail) has no acquisition motion.**

The PMs UP actually targets represent roughly **25% of the total SFR + garden-style PM market by count.** The long tail is the majority by PM count, with smaller average door counts per PM but more PMs in total.

### Why the 100-door cutoff

The current go-to-market motion is BDR-driven outbound. Below 100 doors, the unit economics of personalized outbound do not work — too many accounts, too little revenue per account to justify human-driven sales effort.

**The cutoff is operational, not strategic.** It reflects the constraints of the current motion, not a judgment that smaller PMs are unworthy of attention. A self-serve motion would in principle make the sub-50-door segment economical.

### Modeled scenario: 25% penetration of third-party PMs

- ~50,000 partners (10x today's ~500)
- 35% purchase rate (vs today's ~25%)
- Add residual revenue program
- → significant business

### DIY landlord segment

~10 million homes. Unclear how many use software. Many use software for syndication (listing distribution) but not deeper-funnel features like rent collection or move-in workflows. About 1/3 of expected revenue comes from this segment in current modeling.

## Strategic options

- **Go wide.** Both renters and homeowners, all segments.
- **Go deep.** Dominate one segment first, then expand monetization (energy, other utilities, additional renter products).

## Geographic focus

**Primary markets: Texas and Chicagoland.** Other markets are pursued opportunistically rather than systematically. Sunbelt is the keyword for SFR outbound.

## Ideal Customer Profile

### The decision maker

- Property manager or business owner
- Middle-aged
- Low risk tolerance
- Fears: losing investors, looking bad publicly in front of peers (reputation matters in real estate)
- Long-term goals: financial freedom, work-life balance
- Wants to rein in the chaos of property management (50 tasks a day)
- Used to be tech-averse, now all-in on automation
- Still a bit off with tech — uses Windows, might forget how to start a Zoom call. Grew up before tech was native, but now a believer.

### Team composition (depends on size)

- **Very small:** 1–2 person team, doing it all themselves
- **Bigger:** Leasing agent, international VAs, sometimes a salesperson closing new owners

Active on Facebook and social media.

### The company

- **Average size:** 400 homes
- **Sweet spot:** 100–850 homes (operational sweet spot for the BDR motion)
- **Above 850:** too many decision makers involved
- **Type:** mom-and-pop, typically family-owned
- **Property types:** SFR detached, duplexes through 4-plexes. Occasionally small apartments.

### How they make money

- **Management fees:** 10% of one month's rent
- **Leasing fees:** $0 up to 100% of first month's rent

Low margin because people-heavy. $250K–$500K/year in revenue. Owners can do well, depending on door count.

### Trade associations

About 1/8 of PMs are in a trade association. NARPM is the main one. Newer PMs are most likely to be members (they're learning the business).

### Org structure

- Broker/owner (sometimes same person)
- Property manager (always present)
- Coordinator / vendor manager / technician (often blended into one role)
- Investors/clients (the landlords)
- Residents
- Third-party vendors and technicians for repairs

## How leads and accounts are segmented in HubSpot

- **Door count** — primary segmentation lever
- **Job title** — owner-operator, operations lead, leasing manager, etc.
- **Lead source** — direct mail, cold email, LinkedIn, conference, referral, Peter Lohmann campaign, etc.
- **Geography** — Texas / Chicagoland / other

HubSpot has been overhauled recently with ~28K company records and ~16K SFR contacts to support cleaner segmentation.

## Beyond SFR and garden-style

- **Multifamily** — gated by Yardi integration. Until UP integrates Yardi, much of MF is structurally unreachable.
- **Title companies** — currently paused; see [Title Motion](/mary-playbook/playbook/title-motion/).
- **Direct to consumer** — covers renters, home buyers, second-time home buyers, small landlords, and small businesses opening new locations. See [D2C Motion](/mary-playbook/playbook/d2c-motion/).
- **Real estate brokerages** — theorized as a better partner channel for reaching home buyers than title, but not active.

## Key numbers to remember

- 15M SFR homes
- 1/3 third-party managed
- 2/3 DIY landlords
- ~4x more homes are owner-occupied, but they move less often and need HOA-level relationships to influence
- ~7.8% current SFR market share; 25% is the goal (denominator question is open — see [Strategic Disagreements](/mary-playbook/operating-system/strategic-disagreements/))
