Most objections boil down to "I already have something" or "I don't trust this." The competitors come in three flavors: concierge call centers, bundled resident benefits, and frenemy PM software.

## The competitive landscape, by flavor

### Call-center concierges

**Citizen Home Solutions (CHS), Utility Valet.** Third-party call center concierges. They're call-center led, not tech-forward. The entire model is built around getting the renter on the phone. Once they're on, the rep walks them through setting up utilities — and usually tries to layer in other services (home security, satellite TV, moving services) along the way.

Why this is misaligned with renters today:
- Renters don't want to get on the phone
- They don't want to talk to a stranger
- They just want to know what they're responsible for and get it done quickly

These vendors are vulnerable. PMs typically have low adoption (often only 4–5 out of 10 move-ins actually use the service) and the PM still has an internal process anyway.

### Bundled resident benefits packages

**Second Nature.** Bundled resident benefits — not a dedicated utility solution. Started as a filter delivery company, expanded into bundles that include:
- Filter delivery
- Identity protection / rewards programs
- Renters insurance
- Move-in concierge (utility setup)

Their utility setup is **not native** — it's powered by a third-party call center (historically CHS). As of late 2025 the service is more tightly integrated but fundamentally:
- Still a third-party, outsourced experience
- Optional within the package
- Not operationally embedded into the PM's workflow

Many of our partners still use Second Nature — they just opt out of the utility concierge piece and use us for that portion since it's more embedded into their process.

**Don't replace Second Nature. Replace the weakest part of it.**

### Frenemy PM software (utility features)

**AppFolio's utility tool.** Launched recently. At a high level it's similar to an early beta version of what we originally built.

How AppFolio's tool works:
1. Renter is shown a page explaining they're responsible for utilities
2. Renter still has to leave AppFolio, call the provider or visit their website, and set up the account on their own
3. Renter returns to AppFolio and manually enters the account number or uploads a screenshot to verify

> None of it is actually validated.

The PM still has to review, verify, and chase down missing or inaccurate details. AppFolio communicates responsibility. **We complete the work.**

Other PM software building competing features: this is the gradual market shift that softened our demo close rate from 60–70% historically to ~55% today.

### Other competitors to know

- **SimpleBills** — utility billing, not setup
- **Conservice** — multifamily utility expense management
- **Zego / PayLease** — payment processing with utility adjacencies
- **SmartMove** — TransUnion product, tenant screening with adjacencies

Each is a different shape of competitor. Worth a deeper page per competitor over time.

## Objection: "We use [competitor]"

The playbook is the same shape regardless of which competitor: **gauge sentiment first, then expose the gap.**

### When they say "we use AppFolio's utility tool"

Acknowledge, then surface the gap with a question:

> Totally makes sense. I can definitely see the appeal of wanting everything to live inside AppFolio. Out of curiosity though, once the renter gets to that step, are they still having to go set everything up on their own and then come back and manually enter the info or upload proof?

That question gets them to acknowledge the setup is still manual.

### When they say "we use Second Nature"

Don't position against the full bundle. Isolate utilities as a non-core, low-adoption service:

> Nice, we actually work with quite a few partners who use Second Nature. How long have you been using them?
>
> Got it, out of their offerings, which ones are you currently providing to your residents?
>
> Out of curiosity, how often are your renters actually using that service? So out of 10 renters, how many of them use the third party utility set up service?

Usually 4–5 or they don't know. Then:

> Many of our partners still use Second Nature, they just opt out of the utility concierge piece and use us for that portion since it's more embedded into their process.

### When they say "we use CHS / Utility Valet"

Same pattern:

> Ok got it, how long have you been using them?
>
> Out of like 10 move-ins, how many renters actually complete their utility setups through them?
>
> What about the other half? How are you handling those renters or verifying utilities are set up?

The half they don't get is your opening. Then the line that lands:

> What we've found is renters don't want to get on the phone, they just want to know what they need to do and knock it out quickly. That's why so many groups move to us. We automate the entire process, no calls required.

## Other common objections

- **"It sounds too good to be free."** Walk through revenue model: utility providers pay us a referral commission, we share with you, neither side pays. No contract.
- **"Our tenants won't use this."** Show purchase rate (~25% of addresses). Walk through what we do for the other 75% (no penalty, PM can still manually verify, no friction).
- **"We don't have time to onboard another tool."** 15-minute setup. Most of the work is on us — we map every address with the right utilities before you go live.
- **"What about move-out?"** Acknowledge — we don't solve it yet. It's hard at the data and provider layer. Real product opportunity for whoever cracks it.

## Where there's more room

- **A real objection library, by audience.** Today objections are tribal knowledge in AE heads and the playbook. A queryable library tied to actual reply data would be more powerful.
- **Per-competitor landing pages.** "Utility Profit vs Second Nature" / "vs CHS" / "vs AppFolio utilities" — high-intent SEO targets that don't exist yet.
- **Mining the reply data.** Hundreds of classified replies in the MQL engine carry the actual language prospects use to push back. That's a goldmine for copy and competitive positioning.
