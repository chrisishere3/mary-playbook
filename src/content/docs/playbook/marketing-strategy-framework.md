UP's marketing function operates across four strategic pillars. There's a known gap between stated priority and where time actually goes.

## The four pillars

### 1. BDR support and enablement

**Stated highest-priority area.** The thesis: small percentage improvements in BDR-team performance produce larger total impact than net-new marketing work, because the sales team generates the significant majority of demos.

What this currently includes:

- Comprehensive lists of property management companies for outbound
- Personalization tools (the build-a-system)
- Landing pages tied to outbound campaigns
- Lead enrichment
- Better targeting
- Sales enablement materials

What this does not currently include but probably should:

- Outbound content (overlap with sales enablement, but not built out)
- Organized partner testimonials and references — e.g., door-count-matched references for BDRs to deploy ("we have a partner with a similar door count in your area; here's what they said; you can talk to them")
- A formal reference customer program with some kind of incentive

### 2. Direct performance marketing

Where actual time is spent, despite being #2 in stated priority. Blocking-and-tackling on existing channels:

- Conferences
- Cold email
- Direct mail
- Influencer sponsorships and relationships

See [Channels](/mary-playbook/playbook/channels/) for the per-channel detail.

### 3. New channel zero-to-one

Startup-style hand-to-hand combat to figure out messaging, positioning, channels, and onboarding motion for new segments. Current focus:

- [Title companies](/mary-playbook/playbook/title-motion/) (currently paused)
- [D2C](/mary-playbook/playbook/d2c-motion/) — home buyers and renters

### 4. Moonshots

Vibe-coded projects intended to seed future products or acquisition motions. Examples:

- Utility lookup tool
- Self-serve flow
- Move-out dashboard and vacancy AI caller

No formal owner. "Whoever feels like building one" builds one. See [Architect Workflow](/mary-playbook/playbook/architect-workflow/) for how non-engineers ship these.

## Time vs. stated priority

There is a known gap between stated priority and actual time allocation.

- Stated #1 is BDR support. **Actual #1 by time is direct performance marketing.**
- Moonshot time gets more energy than the framework would justify.

The VP needs to either (a) realign time to match stated priority, or (b) update the stated priority to match where time actually goes. Both are defensible. **The current ambiguity is not.**

## What is explicitly out of scope today

Marketing currently does not own:

- Brand
- PR
- Product marketing (attempted with tenant liability insurance and bulk internet launches; no muscle developed)
- Customer marketing (Cassie on AM handles retention, but it does not operate as a growth function)
- Community

All of the above could reasonably fall under marketing — or, more accurately, under growth — and will likely fall under the VP's purview.

## Implicit org allocation today

- Mark: SFR + D2C (primary focus), with overflow into moonshots
- Chris: garden-style multifamily + SFR (tag-teamed with Mark)
- Both: "keeping the lights on" with SFR/GS while standing up title and D2C
- Moonshot ownership: none (opt-in)
- Cross-team channel work (direct mail, cold email, etc.): shared informally with no designated owner per channel

## Prioritization mechanism

There is no formal weekly prioritization between the senior ICs. They talk to the team and decide what to focus on. **Speed is high; accountability against the stated strategy is low.**

## How the VP should approach the framework

The team welcomes discipline. Clarify what's actually #1, get time aligned with stated priority, and make the implicit explicit. The current autonomy is fine to disrupt.
